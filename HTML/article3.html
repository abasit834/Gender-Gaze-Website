Breaking Chains

The Quest for Equal Gender Morality in Pakistan

As a male witnessing the landscape of Pakistan’s culture, I am profoundly aware of the glaring imbalance that persists within our societal fabric, specifically the unequal treatment of Pakistani women. The knotty web of moral standards in our society often emits different lights on the behaviors and actions of men and women. Suppose a close examination is given to our society's complex web of norms. In that case, it will definitely reveal a stark reality. In this discussion, I want to highlight the ongoing gender inequalities, particularly focusing on moral aspects. I'll use examples from Pakistani society to illustrate how women face these challenges. Let's explore the tangible barriers women encounter within our complex societal expectations.

Pakistan, a nation filled with the richness of traditions and cultures, struggles with ingrained perceptions about gender morality and roles. The societal framework in which we are living, women often find themselves obstructed by rigid moral standards that differ distinctly from those which imposed upon men.

In my surroundings, men tend to have greater freedom in making choices about their careers, social interactions, and education. At the same time, women often encounter societal constraints throughout their entire lives. The expectation of chastity, modesty, and adherence to traditional roles put considerable limitations on the autonomy of women, which shapes their morality as with prescribed lines.

Sadly, in Pakistan, a woman's behavior is often linked to the idea of honor, shaping her moral conduct and restricting her freedom. This societal pressure can be suffocating, as the strict codes of honor heavily influence how women are expected to behave. The burden of societal reputation and preserving familial honor is frequently placed on women disproportionately, which restricts their choices, actions, and free will.

Highlighting the gender equality for women within our Pakistani Society:

  • Differential Assumptions of Modesty: Women often face strict expectations for their clothing choices, which are subjected to intense scrutiny. On the other hand, men typically face fewer restrictions for their social behavior and attire. For example, a woman wearing Western-style clothes would be shamed or judged, unlike men’s clothing whichrarely raises these concerns.

  • Limitations on Education and Career: While the education sector in Pakistan has seen advancements, women still face obstacles in pursuing higher education and specific career paths. There's a visible trend where families prioritize educating their male children over females. This bias often leads to statements like 'She'll just end up getting married anyway,' disregarding that marriage doesn't define a woman's entire life. This mindset can stem from parents seeing their sons as future support or investments for their old age while assuming their daughters will marry into another family. As a result, unequal educational opportunities limit women's access to economic independence and hinder their professional growth.

  • Double Standards and Moral Policing: Double Standards and Moral Policing: Society frequently applies moral standards on women's social interactions and conduct. For example, a woman’s character will be questioned with rumors or she might face censure for socializing with her male friends whereas a men with similar interactions will be ignored or even praised!!! These double standards puts women’s freedom in a cage of stifling restrictions.

  • Violence against Women and Honor Culture: These cases can be witnessed frequently in the society of Pakistan, such as a brother killing his sister, a husband killing his wife, father killing his daughter for honor. Why? Who granted them the authority to dictate life and death? Women bear the severe consequences of enduring domestic violence or even facing honor killings when their actions challenge these norms imposed by the male-dominated society. But it begs the question: By what right does this male society claim to be the arbiter of judgment? Who are they to set these parameters and pass such harsh judgments?

Moreover, the societal gaze tends to moralize and scrutinize the appearances and actions of women rigorously. From attire to social interactions, from choices to will, from education to professional life, women face moral policing as compared to men.

In conclusion, the struggle is real for equality in gender morality within the society of Pakistan, which has various aspects required to be battled against the perceptions and norms. Empowering women while embracing diversity to foster inclusivity within our culture should be prioritized for providing the steps for weaving the most likely moral tapestry equitably where any gender cannot be dictated by anyone’s measure of their own morality.
